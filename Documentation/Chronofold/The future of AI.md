## The AI Bubble Won't End in Extinction. It Will End in a "Cognitive Class Divide."

There is a lot of talk right now about the existential threat of AI—the idea that an Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) will "wake up" and inevitably clash with human interests.

But as someone who uses these frontier models heavily, I am not losing sleep over sci-fi extinction scenarios. The math of economics and physics is staring us in the face, and it tells a much more grounded—and perhaps more alarming—story.

If we want to understand where AI is heading, we shouldn't look at Terminator. We should look at the Apollo Project and the Dot-com Bubble.

### 1. The Apollo Illusion

The current AI race perfectly mirrors the Space Age. Big Tech is spending hundreds of billions of dollars on a monolithic, grand vision: achieving AGI before the end of the decade. It is the modern equivalent of "putting a man on the moon."

But just like the Apollo project, this sprint is incredibly resource-intensive. The primary bottlenecks for AI aren't algorithmic; they are physical and economic.

- **The Energy Wall:** Data centers are eating global electricity grids at an unsustainable rate.
    
- **The Cost of Inference:** Training a model is a one-time sunk cost, but running it for millions of users daily is bleeding companies dry.
    

Eventually, just like the lunar missions, the broader market will realize that the "omni-capable" AGI is simply too expensive to sustain for the general public.

### 2. The Dot-Com Destiny

So, what happens when the AGI bubble bursts? It won't be a catastrophic bankruptcy of Big Tech, but rather a "fizzle out" mirroring the Dot-com crash of 2000.

During the late 90s, telecom companies spent billions laying "dark fiber" cables across the globe. When the bubble burst, the speculative companies died, but that fiber-optic infrastructure remained—giving us the cheap bandwidth that built the modern internet.

The AI market will likely do the same. Big Tech is currently overbuilding massive GPU clusters. When the hype cools, we won't get a conscious superintelligence, but we _will_ get incredibly cheap, highly specialized "satellite" AIs. These narrow tools will quietly revolutionize medicine, logistics, and coding, weaving themselves into the background of the economy.

### 3. The $300/Month Power User

This leads to the most immediate shift in how we will interact with AI.

Right now, we are living in a heavily subsidized window where $20 a month buys you access to supercomputing power. That will end. To become profitable, AI companies will gate their most powerful, context-aware models behind steep enterprise paywalls.

In the near future, the frictionless, omni-capable AI will cost $200 to $300 a month. It won't need to be prompted; it will sit in your operating system, learn your tone, read your live workload, and act as a tireless senior partner.

### 4. The Real Shift: Cognitive Inequality and an Elevated Baseline

This is where the true friction of the AI era lies. It is not a robot uprising; it is a new class system.

We are about to see the rise of the **AI Divide**. Unlike the "Digital Divide," which was a gap in access to _information_, this is a gap in _cognitive leverage_.

The power user who can afford—and knows how to orchestrate—a $300/month frontier model will effectively command the raw intellectual output of ten highly skilled professionals. The person who cannot afford that amplification, or relies on the downgraded, localized AI of the future, will initially be left competing in a fundamentally unfair market. We are heading toward a society sharply divided between the amplified and the un-amplified.

But if we widen the lens, history offers a deeply optimistic counterweight.

The Apollo and Dot-com eras both proved that the massive, expensive infrastructure built during a bubble never truly vanishes—it eventually democratizes. The cognitive elite paying a premium will forge the bleeding edge, but the "dark fiber" of this AI boom—the highly specialized, deeply integrated models running our medical diagnostics, optimizing our grids, and operating locally on our devices—will permanently elevate the baseline of human capability.

We are navigating a turbulent economic transition, not a sci-fi dystopia. The gap will be jarring at first, but on the other side of it, the floor for what humanity can achieve will be higher than ever before.
